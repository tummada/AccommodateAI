## Timeliness

### ADA Timeliness: "As Quickly As Possible"

The ADA does not set a hard statutory deadline, but EEOC guidance and case law establish that employers must act "as quickly as possible." For SMB practical implementation:

| Step | SMB Timeline | Notes |
|------|-------------|-------|
| Acknowledge request | 1-3 business days | Written acknowledgment to employee that request was received |
| Initiate interactive process | 5-7 business days | Schedule first meeting/conversation with employee |
| Request medical documentation (if needed) | Within 7 business days of initiating interactive process | Limited scope — functional limitations only |
| Complete interactive process | 15-30 business days from request | Depends on complexity; document reason for any delay |
| Implement approved accommodation | Within 5-10 business days of approval | Longer if equipment procurement needed — document timeline |

### PWFA Timeliness: "Expeditious" (Legally Stricter)

PWFA uses the term "expeditious" which courts interpret as stricter than ADA's standard. Pregnancy is time-limited, so delays are more legally dangerous.

| Step | SMB Timeline | Notes |
|------|-------------|-------|
| Acknowledge request | Same day or next business day | PWFA cases are time-sensitive due to pregnancy duration |
| Predictable assessment (4 auto-approvals) | Approve on receipt — same day | No interactive process needed, no medical docs |
| Initiate interactive process (non-predictable) | 3-5 business days | Faster than ADA because pregnancy progresses |
| Complete interactive process | 10-15 business days | Must account for changing needs as pregnancy progresses |
| Implement accommodation | Within 3-5 business days of approval | Delay = potential violation given time-limited nature |

### Delay Documentation Requirement (Both ADA and PWFA)

If ANY step takes longer than the timeline above, HR must document:
1. Specific reason for delay (e.g., waiting for medical provider response, equipment on backorder)
2. Date HR communicated the delay to employee
3. Revised expected timeline communicated to employee
4. Interim measures provided (if any) while waiting

## Reassignment

### Reassignment to Vacant Position — ADA Accommodation of Last Resort

Reassignment is the accommodation of last resort under the ADA. It is triggered ONLY when:
1. The employee can no longer perform essential functions of current position WITH any reasonable accommodation
2. All other effective accommodations have been explored and exhausted
3. A vacant equivalent or lower-level position exists

### Rules:
- **Position must be vacant** — employer cannot displace (bump) another employee to create the vacancy
- **Employee must be qualified** — must meet the minimum qualifications for the new position (education, experience, skills)
- **Equivalent or lower level** — employer is not required to promote the employee; reassignment is to an equivalent or lower-level position
- **Not required if no vacancy exists** — employer has no obligation to create a new position
- **Competitive vs. non-competitive:** EEOC guidance says reassignment should be non-competitive (employee gets the position without competing against other applicants), though some circuits disagree — flag for legal review
- **Pay and benefits:** if reassigned to a lower-level position, employer may reduce pay to match the new position's pay scale

### SMB Workflow:
1. Document that all other accommodations were considered and why each failed
2. Search current open positions for vacancies employee is qualified for
3. If vacancy found — offer reassignment to employee
4. If employee accepts — implement transfer with new accommodation plan if needed
5. If no vacancy — document search performed and result; continue monitoring for future openings for a reasonable period
6. If employee declines suitable vacancy — document offer and declination; employer has fulfilled obligation

## Leave Anti-Pattern

### Leave-as-Default: Top EEOC-Cited Employer Failure Pattern

The EEOC consistently identifies "defaulting to unpaid leave" as one of the most common employer failures. Placing an employee on leave when they could continue working with a workplace adjustment violates ADA requirements.

### Required Workflow — Exhaust Work Modifications First:

1. **Schedule/hours modification** — flexible start/end times, compressed workweek, part-time transition
2. **Workspace modification** — ergonomic equipment, lighting, noise reduction, location change
3. **Job duty modification** — reassign marginal functions, modify how essential functions are performed
4. **Policy modification** — break schedule, dress code, food/drink rules, attendance flexibility
5. **Assistive technology** — screen readers, speech-to-text, specialized software
6. **Remote work / telework** — if in-person attendance is not a documented essential function
7. **Reassignment** — to a vacant equivalent or lower-level position (last resort — see Reassignment section)
8. **ONLY THEN: Leave** — if no work modification enables the employee to perform essential functions

### ADA Obligations After FMLA Exhaustion:

- When an employee's 12 weeks of FMLA leave expire, ADA obligations do NOT end
- Employer must re-enter the interactive process to determine if additional leave or another accommodation would enable the employee to return
- Auto-terminating an employee at FMLA exhaustion without ADA interactive process = high litigation risk
- Extended leave beyond FMLA may be a reasonable accommodation under ADA unless it creates undue hardship
- System must: flag cases approaching FMLA exhaustion — auto-trigger ADA interactive process review at week 10 of FMLA

## Revocation Protocol

### Revoking or Modifying a Previously Granted Accommodation

An employer cannot simply withdraw an accommodation. Revocation requires a structured process:

### Triggering Events for Re-evaluation:
- Employee's job duties change (promotion, transfer, reorganization)
- Workload or operational needs change significantly
- New medical information from employee or provider
- Accommodation is no longer effective (employee or employer reports)
- Position is eliminated or restructured

### Required Steps:
1. **Document the trigger** — record specifically what changed and why the current accommodation may no longer be viable
2. **Re-initiate the interactive process** — schedule meeting with employee to discuss the change
3. **Explain the situation** — inform employee of the specific reason the accommodation is being reconsidered
4. **Invite employee alternatives** — give employee the opportunity to propose alternative accommodations before any revocation takes effect
5. **Evaluate alternatives** — assess each proposed alternative using the same undue hardship framework
6. **Select new accommodation or confirm revocation** — if no effective alternative exists, document why
7. **Provide transition period** — give employee reasonable time to adjust (minimum 2 weeks unless safety concern)
8. **Document everything** — final decision rationale, employee input received, alternatives considered

### What Employer CANNOT Do:
- Revoke accommodation without any process (immediate removal)
- Revoke because a new manager "doesn't agree" with the accommodation
- Revoke as retaliation for complaint, performance feedback, or leave usage
- Revoke without giving employee the chance to propose alternatives

## State Laws

### State Laws Exceeding ADA/PWFA Federal Floors

| State | Law | Key Differences from Federal |
|-------|-----|------------------------------|
| California | FEHA (Fair Employment and Housing Act) | Covers employers with **5+ employees** (vs. ADA's 15+). Broader disability definition: includes "limitation" not just "substantial limitation." Requires interactive process even if employer believes no accommodation is needed. Covers more conditions (e.g., HIV/AIDS explicitly). |
| New York City | NYCHRL (NYC Human Rights Law) | Covers employers with **4+ employees.** No undue hardship defense for **mental disability** accommodations — employer must accommodate unless it creates a "direct threat." Broader definition of disability than ADA. Cooperative dialogue (interactive process) is mandatory. |
| New Jersey | LAD (Law Against Discrimination) | Covers **all employers regardless of size** (no employee minimum). Broader disability definition than ADA. Includes perceived disability. Interactive process required by case law. |
| Illinois | IHRA (Illinois Human Rights Act) | Covers employers with **15+ employees** (same as ADA) but includes additional protected classes. Broader pregnancy accommodation requirements pre-dating PWFA. |
| Massachusetts | Chapter 151B | Covers employers with **6+ employees.** Includes protections for employees with criminal records that may intersect with accommodation requests. |

### Trigger Condition for State-Specific Legal Review:

System must flag a case for acmd-legal state-specific review when ANY of these conditions are true:
1. Employee's work state is California, New York (City or State), New Jersey, Illinois, or Massachusetts
2. Employer size is below ADA threshold (15 employees) but above state threshold
3. Accommodation request involves mental health disability (NYCHRL has different rules)
4. Employee's state is not in the system's state law database (unknown = flag for review)
5. Case involves multi-state employment (remote worker in different state than employer HQ)

## EEOC Defense Package

### Minimum EEOC Charge-Defense Documentation Package

When an employee files an EEOC charge, the employer must produce documentation proving good-faith compliance. Every completed accommodation case must contain AT MINIMUM these 5 items:

| # | Document | Required Content | System Implementation |
|---|----------|-----------------|----------------------|
| 1 | Dated accommodation request record | Date received, how received (written/verbal/email), exact language used by employee, who received it, when forwarded to HR | Auto-generated on case creation; verbal requests documented by HR same day |
| 2 | Interactive process log | Timestamp + participant names for every exchange (meeting, email, phone call); what was discussed; what was agreed/disagreed | System logs every interaction with timestamp; participants confirmed at each step |
| 3 | Accommodation options considered | List of all options explored (from JAN, employee suggestion, employer suggestion), reason each was accepted OR rejected | Checklist item — system blocks case closure if options not documented |
| 4 | Final decision rationale | If approved: which accommodation and why selected. If denied: undue hardship analysis tied to 4-factor test, or essential functions assessment | Linked to undue hardship form (if denial) or approval record |
| 5 | Employee notification | Date notification sent to employee, delivery method (email/letter/in-person), content of notification (what was decided), employee's acknowledged receipt if available | System generates notification template; tracks send date and delivery confirmation |

### System Enforcement:
- Case CANNOT be marked "resolved" unless all 5 documents are present
- Missing any document triggers compliance warning to HR admin
- All documents must be stored in accommodation case file, separate from general personnel file
- Retention period: minimum 3 years after case resolution (EEOC statute of limitations + buffer)

## Multi-Law Classification

### Classification Logic for Cases Qualifying Under Multiple Laws

A single accommodation request may trigger obligations under multiple laws simultaneously. The system must identify ALL applicable frameworks and apply them concurrently.

### Common Multi-Law Scenarios:

| Scenario | Applicable Laws | Which Governs |
|----------|----------------|---------------|
| Pregnant employee with depression | ADA (mental disability) + PWFA (pregnancy-related condition) + state FMLA | Most protective standard for each aspect |
| Employee with disability requesting leave | ADA (reasonable accommodation) + FMLA (if eligible) + state FMLA | FMLA for leave entitlement, ADA for accommodation beyond FMLA |
| Employee in California with chronic condition | ADA + FEHA + CFRA (state FMLA) | FEHA broader definition applies; CFRA for leave |
| Pregnant employee in NJ needing schedule change | PWFA + NJ LAD + NJ FLA | NJ LAD covers all employer sizes; all frameworks apply |

### Decision Logic:
1. **Identify all potentially applicable laws** — ADA, PWFA, FMLA, state disability law, state FMLA, local ordinances
2. **For each aspect of the case** (definition of covered condition, interactive process requirements, available accommodations, employer defenses), determine which law is most protective of the employee
3. **Apply the most protective standard for each aspect** — it is not always the same law for every aspect
4. **Document which law governs each decision point** — this is critical for EEOC defense
5. **Flag for acmd-legal review** if more than 2 laws apply simultaneously or if state/local law is involved

### System Implementation:
- Classification engine must check: ADA eligibility + PWFA eligibility + employee work-state laws + FMLA eligibility
- Display all applicable frameworks on case dashboard
- Checklist must include steps from ALL applicable frameworks (union of requirements)
- Cannot close case until compliance verified under each applicable law

## Remote Work Accommodation

### Remote Work / Telework as Standalone ADA Accommodation

Remote work is a recognized reasonable accommodation under the ADA, separate from general company remote work policy.

### Evaluation Test:

| Question | If Yes | If No |
|----------|--------|-------|
| Is regular in-person attendance explicitly listed and documented as an essential function of this specific position? | Denial may be justified — but must still consider partial remote (hybrid) | Remote work should be strongly considered as accommodation |
| Does the employee's actual work require physical presence (e.g., operating machinery, in-person customer service)? | Remote work may not be feasible — document why | Remote work is likely a reasonable accommodation |
| Have other employees in this role or similar roles worked remotely (including during pandemic)? | Precedent weakens "essential function" argument for in-person attendance | Employer position is stronger but not dispositive |
| Can essential functions be performed remotely with technology accommodations? | Remote work is likely reasonable | Document specific functions that require in-person presence |

### Post-Pandemic Case Law (2024-2026):

- Courts have increasingly scrutinized blanket return-to-office mandates applied to employees with disabilities
- If employer allowed remote work during COVID-19 for the same position, arguing in-person attendance is now "essential" is significantly harder
- EEOC has issued updated guidance (2024) emphasizing that remote work must be considered as an accommodation on a case-by-case basis
- Employer must provide individualized assessment — blanket "everyone must be in office" policies do not satisfy ADA interactive process requirements

### SMB Implementation:
1. When employee requests remote work as accommodation, do NOT apply general company remote work policy
2. Evaluate whether in-person attendance is a documented essential function of THIS position (not "company culture" or "collaboration preference")
3. If essential function argument is weak — approve remote work (full or partial/hybrid)
4. If essential function argument is strong — document specific tasks requiring presence, explore hybrid schedule, consider other accommodations
5. Document the entire analysis regardless of outcome
6. If denying — mandatory acmd-legal review before communicating denial to employee
